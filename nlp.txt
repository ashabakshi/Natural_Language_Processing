What is Nlp?
-NLP (Natural Language Processing) is a field of AI (Artificial Intelligence) that helps computers understand,
     interpret, and generate human language — like English, Hindi, etc.

In simple words:
👉 It’s how machines “read, listen, and talk” like humans.

Real World Applications of NLP:

            Chatbots: automated user conversation
            Translation: language convert (EN ↔ HI)
            Sentiment Analysis: detect emotion in text
            Voice Assistants: speech understanding
            Spam Detection: filter unwanted emails
            Autocomplete: predict next words
            Search Engines: understand queries
            Text Summarization: short from long text
            Resume Screening: filter candidates
            Text Generation: AI writing (answers/code)




---

**Approaches of NLP*

* Rule-Based Approach:
  Uses fixed grammar rules and dictionaries
  Limitation: cannot handle new or informal text
  👉 Example: “This movie is lit 🔥” → system may not understand “lit”

---

* Statistical Approach:
  Uses word frequency and probability
  Limitation: ignores full meaning/context
  👉 Example: “bank” (river bank vs money bank) → confusion

---

* Machine Learning Approach:
  Learns from labeled data
  Limitation: depends on training data quality
  👉 Example: if trained only on positive reviews → fails on negative ones

---

* Deep Learning Approach:
  Uses neural networks to understand context
  Limitation: needs large data + high power
  👉 Example: small dataset → model gives wrong predictions

---

* Hybrid Approach:
  Combines rules + ML/DL
  Limitation: complex to build/manage
  👉 Example: more time & effort to maintain both systems

**Text Representation Techniques in NLP**

---

**1. One Hot Encoding**

Definition:
Each word is represented as a vector with 0s and 1s (only one 1, rest 0)

Example:
["I", "love", "AI"]
I → [1,0,0]
love → [0,1,0]
AI → [0,0,1]

Pros:

* Simple and easy
* No calculation needed

Cons:

* High dimensional (too many columns)
* No meaning/relationship between words
* Sparse matrix (mostly zeros)

Use when:

* Small dataset
* Basic understanding

Avoid when:

* Large vocabulary
* Need semantic meaning

---

**2. Bag of Words (BoW)**

Definition:
Counts frequency of each word in sentence (order ignored)

Example:
"I love AI love"
→ {I:1, love:2, AI:1}

Pros:

* Simple and effective
* Works well for basic tasks
* Easy to implement

Cons:

* Ignores word order
* No semantic meaning
* High dimensional for large vocab

Use when:

* Text classification (spam, sentiment)
* Small to medium dataset

Avoid when:

* Need context/meaning
* Complex NLP tasks

---

**3. TF-IDF (Term Frequency – Inverse Document Frequency)**

Definition:
Gives importance to words based on frequency in document and rarity in dataset

TF = how often word appears
IDF = how rare word is

Pros:

* Better than BoW
* Reduces importance of common words
* Highlights important words

Cons:

* Still no deep meaning
* Ignores word order
* Slightly complex

Use when:

* Text classification
* Search engines
* Medium datasets

Avoid when:

* Deep understanding needed
* Large complex NLP tasks

---

**Which is Best?**

TF-IDF is generally best among these

Why:

* Considers importance of words
* Reduces noise (common words)
* Better accuracy than BoW and One Hot

---

**Final Summary**

One Hot → simplest but inefficient
BoW → good basic model
TF-IDF → best for traditional ML models

For advanced NLP:
Use Word Embeddings / Transformers
